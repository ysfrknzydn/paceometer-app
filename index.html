About Paceometer

Instead of just showing your speed, Paceometer shows how much time speeding up would actually save you, plus a traffic-light color so you can tell at a glance whether it's still worth it.

The pace number (minutes to cover 10 miles at your current speed) is based on a real published study (Peer & Gamliel, 2013), which found that showing drivers this number instead of just mph made them much better at judging how much time speeding up would actually save.

The traffic light: green means going 10mph faster would still save meaningful time; yellow means that gain is shrinking; red means going faster wouldn't meaningfully save you any time at all. These specific speed cutoffs (around 50mph and 73mph) are this app's own design choice, not from a published study — chosen because they land on clean, round numbers within the same math as the pace formula above.

The end-of-trip number is how many seconds you spent going slower than the fastest pace that would've actually helped, counted only over the parts of the trip where speeding up would have mattered. A small number is a good sign — it means you were already driving close to an efficient pace.

What this app doesn't know: the speed limit or how safe a road is. It only answers the time-savings math above, nothing else — so "green" never means a road is safe to speed on.

Privacy

Your raw location (GPS coordinates) is never sent anywhere or stored — it stays on your phone the whole time, used only to calculate your speed in the moment.

When you end a trip, only summary numbers are saved: average/min/max speed, distance, and how much of the trip was in the "still helps" zone. No route and no coordinates ever leave your phone.

Only you can see your own trips — the database is locked down so each signed-in driver can only read their own data.
